What is MPC?

Multi-Party Computation (MPC) is a branch of cryptography that allows multiple parties to jointly compute a result over their combined data, without any single party ever seeing the other parties' raw inputs.

In plain terms: imagine a group of people need to calculate something together. Normally, one person would collect all the data, do the math, and share the result. MPC removes that central person entirely. The calculation happens across all parties simultaneously, and the result comes out correctly, but no individual ever held the complete picture.

Arcium takes this mathematical concept and builds infrastructure around it at scale. Instead of parties being people, the parties are independent computer nodes distributed across the world. Instead of a single calculation, Arcium runs thousands of computations per second. The principle stays the same: the result is computed. The data is never revealed.

03 ‚Äî What MPC Actually Is

Three People. One Secret. Nobody Knows the Whole Thing.

Here is the simplest version of MPC you will ever read.

Three colleagues want to find out who earns the most, but none of them is willing to say their salary out loud. This is actually a problem. If nobody shares their number, nobody learns anything. If one person shares first, they are vulnerable.

Here is what they do instead. Each person takes their salary and adds a large, completely random number to it before sharing anything. Person A earns 50,000 and privately picks the random number 73,000. They tell Person B the result: 123,000. That number tells Person B absolutely nothing, because the random number buried the real one. Person B does the same with their own salary, adds Person A's number, passes it to Person C. After one full exchange, they run a simple calculation and the result tells them who earns the most, without any individual ever learning any other individual's actual salary.

This thought experiment has a name. It is called the Millionaires Problem, first described by computer scientist Andrew Yao in 1982. The math that solves it turns out to be the foundation of an entire field of cryptography. Multi-Party Computation is that field.

Now bring this closer to home. Across West Africa, communities have practiced a group savings system called susu for generations. A group of people, say ten neighbours, each put in a fixed amount every month. Each month, one member receives the full pot. Every member tracks only their own contributions. No single person ever controls the whole pool.

The community achieves a collective financial outcome, rotating savings, without any single person holding everyone else's money or knowing everyone else's finances. The trust is distributed. No single point of failure. No single person who, if they disappeared, would take everything with them.

That is the human version of what Arcium does at a mathematical level. Distribute the data. Run the computation. Get the result. No single node was ever in possession of the whole picture.

The Math Behind It ‚Äî Shamir's Secret Sharing (1979)

Invented by Israeli cryptographer Adi Shamir, this is the mathematical engine underneath Arcium's secret sharing. Here is how it actually works in simple terms.

Say your secret is the number 42. Arcium does not send 42 to any node. Instead, it builds a polynomial equation where 42 is hidden inside the structure of the curve. It then calculates three specific points on that curve and sends one point to each node. These three numbers are not random. They are mathematically derived from a polynomial that has 42 baked into it:

These are equation-derived values, not random guesses. Each one is a point on a polynomial curve. You need all three points to reconstruct the curve and find the secret at its root. With only two points, you cannot determine the curve. The math makes it impossible. In Arcium, the three nodes never come together in one place, so the secret is never reconstructed during computation.

05 ‚Äî The Compute

The Calculation Runs. The Secret Never Moves.

Picture three separate banks. One in Lagos. One in Paris. One in Berlin. You want a loan. Instead of sending your full financial history to one institution that stores it, reads it, and becomes a target, each bank receives only a scrambled, meaningless fragment of your record. None of the three banks can read what they hold.

But together, using a shared mathematical process agreed upon before the computation begins, they compute whether you qualify. The answer arrives: approved or denied. No bank ever saw your actual salary. No bank could reconstruct your full record from the fragment they held. The computation happened. The privacy held.

This is exactly what Arcium's Arx Nodes do on every computation that runs on the network. Each node holds a keyshare. They run calculations on their own fragment only. The results combine mathematically into a final output. The original data never reassembles in any one place.

Cerberus Protocol ‚Äî How Arcium Stops Cheating

In any system with multiple parties, someone might try to cheat. What stops a rogue node from sending false data and corrupting the result? Arcium's answer is the Cerberus protocol. Here is how it works step by step:

01

Every keyshare gets a cryptographic tag

When a keyshare is created and sent to a node, it travels with a Message Authentication Code (MAC). Think of this as a tamper-proof seal on an envelope. The seal is mathematically linked to the exact value inside.

MAC attached
02

Nodes verify before they accept

Before any node uses a keyshare in a computation, it checks the MAC. If the value inside matches the seal, the share is legitimate and computation proceeds.

Verification passes
03

Tampering is mathematically impossible to hide

If a rogue node changes the value of its keyshare, the MAC seal breaks. You cannot change the content and keep the seal valid. The other nodes detect this instantly.

Tamper detected
04

The rogue node gets penalised

The computation aborts. The rogue node is identified. A slashing mechanism cuts into the ARX tokens they have staked as collateral. Cheating is costly. Honesty is the rational choice.
